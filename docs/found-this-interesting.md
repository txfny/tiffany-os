## 2026-06-24 — Trauma, implicit memory, and "I can't remember half my life"

**Source:** TikTok by @imcxlebb + comment from @sabrinacasale_therapy (therapist)

**The clip:** guy says he can only recall most of his life if someone else brings
the memory up first — riffing on a girl's post about dissociating so hard she
doesn't remember half her life.

**The therapist's explanation:**
- Memory gaps like this often trace back to childhood trauma — not just physical
  abuse, also emotional neglect, a parent with mental health issues, high-conflict
  households.
- Under extreme/chronic stress, the amygdala (threat) and hippocampus (memory)
  can temporarily disconnect, so memories get stored as "implicit" fragments —
  sensation/emotion instead of a coherent storybook narrative.
- A disproportionate emotional reaction as an adult can be the body
  "remembering" — e.g. feeling powerless now triggers the same response as
  feeling powerless back then, because the body can't tell the timeline apart.
- A smell, sound, or physical sensation can act like a key that unlocks a
  fragment the brain was holding onto.

**My read:** mechanism checks out. Cortisol can suppress hippocampal (explicit/
verbal) encoding while the amygdala keeps tagging things emotionally — that's
the real basis of van der Kolk's "the body keeps the score." State-dependent
retrieval is real too: the body re-enters the physiological state it encoded
in, and that state is the door back in. One nuance: not every memory gap means
trauma — sleep deprivation, ADHD, and dissociation-on-a-spectrum can all blank
stuff out too. The comment said "typically," not "always," so it held that
line better than most trauma-TikTok.

**Why it's worth keeping here:** this is basically the conceptual core of the
nervous-system module (still `planned`) — HRV etc. are proxies for exactly this
implicit/somatic signal layer.
