step 2 of 7 · the blend

attention isn't a spotlight, it's a weighted blend.

we usually picture attention as a spotlight: one thing lit, everything else dark. it isn't.

at any moment, you're 70% on the text, 20% on the body, 10% somewhere else, drifting. the weights are always shifting, mostly without your say. they sum to roughly 100% — what gets more, the rest gets less.

step 4 of 7 · model mechanism

now a transformer.

when the model wants to predict the next token, every previous token gets three vectors:

— a query (Q): what is this position asking about?
— a key (K): what does this token represent?
— a value (V): what's this token's content to deliver?

the current position's Q gets dotted with every previous token's K. high dot product = high relevance. then softmax-normalize those scores into weights summing to 1, and pull a weighted sum of the V's.

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √d) · V

that's the entire mechanism. attention is literally a similarity search across the context, with the answer being a blend of values weighted by relevance.

step 5 of 7 · family resemblance

plainly: both systems do weighted blends.

different scale, different substrate, same shape.

you

  • weighted inputs from sight, sound, body, memory, thought
  • top-down (prefrontal) goal + bottom-up (sensory) salience
  • thousands of nerve channels per millisecond
  • thalamus gates what cortex sees

the model

  • weighted tokens from the context window
  • Q from the current position, K/V from prior positions
  • hundreds to thousands of tokens at once
  • softmax gates how much each token contributes

where the analogy breaks

  • in your brain, attention shapes what gets encoded — you tend to remember what you attended to
  • in a transformer, attention is computed only at inference; the weights don't change the model
  • you walk away from a conversation changed. the model doesn't. yet.
step 6 of 7 · for your work

a quick aside — because you build agents.

when you're designing an agentic RAG system, you're shaping what the model attends to in the most literal sense.

your retrieval step selects the K's and V's (the candidate chunks). your prompt is the Q (what's being asked). the model's attention layer is what decides which retrieved chunks influence the answer.

the load-bearing insight

most RAG bugs are attention bugs. irrelevant chunks with high embedding similarity grab too much weight. relevant chunks with low similarity get ignored. the embedding model and the retrieval ranker are the K/V provisioning layer for the model's attention. they decide what the model can attend to.

when you debug an HR-Assist style mismatch — "why did it surface this candidate?" — you're debugging the attention layer's K/V inputs, even if the symptoms show up downstream. internalizing this might change how you think about retrieval quality.

step 7 of 7 · sit with

one thing you can do that the model can't.

you can't control where your attention goes most of the time. it's pulled by salience — what's loudest, most relevant, most threatening. a transformer can't either; its weighting is fixed at training.

but you can do one thing the model can't yet: you can notice that you're attending.

the noticing is itself a kind of meta-attention — attention turned on attention. it's the thing we don't know how to build. it might be the thing that matters most.
